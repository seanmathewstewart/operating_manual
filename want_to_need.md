
# How to Get from Problem to Solution

## What You Need to Know About the Business
They always know what they **_want_**…
- …but they never know what they **_need_**
- …and everything is **_urgent_**

## Your Job
Your job is to take their *wants* and translate them into concrete *needs*.
- This will be the hardest part of your job!

## How to Get from 'Wants' to 'Needs'?
Ask these types of **open-ended** questions:
- What are you trying to do?
- What problem are you trying to solve?
- Describe the problem?
- What question are you trying to answer?
- What will you do with the answer?
- If we delivered this tomorrow, when would you use it?
- What is this preventing you from doing?
- Where is the pain?

## Lean Into the “So What”?
There are many, many, many, many problems at Sana.
How do you know if this problem is worth solving?

You need to ask **“So what?”**

If solving the problem doesn’t:
- Improve something by 20%
- Allow us to operate faster
- Simplify something that is complex

…then don’t do it. Say no! You’ve got better things to do.

## How Do You Know If You Succeeded?
- You can describe the problem they are trying to solve in plain English.
- It often takes several iterations to get there.
- You can answer the question “so what?”

## Solving the Problem
When solving problems, consider:
- **Speed** – How long does it take?
- **Simplicity** – How easy is it to do?
- **Completeness** – Have you thought of every edge case?
- **Stability** – Will this work months into the future?

**Sana should optimize for SPEED and SIMPLICITY.**
Always ask:
- Can we do it simpler?
- Can we do it faster?

## Solving the Problem (Iterative Approach)
Over the years, many solved problems were never used or weren’t as important as we first thought.

It’s best to start small and solve the most immediate problem first. This allows you to:
- Keep solutions simple
- Move faster
- Get the business using what you already built

It also lets you gather feedback and iterate.
**Ship early and ship often!**

## Red Flags
- We can’t describe the problem simply (e.g., to your mother)
- We can’t answer “so what?”
- The stakeholder brings a *solution* instead of a *problem*

## Example: Churn Analysis 2025 Q4
**The Ask:**
- Sebastiaan requested an urgent analysis for the board
- He specified: “Produce these 4 charts, using these line charts, these dimensions, and these colors”

**Red Flag:**
The business should not bring solutions—they should bring problems.

**My job:**
Find the question behind the question.

**What I delivered:**
- None of what he asked for :)
- Instead, an analysis that answered the questions he *actually* needed answered
- He was very happy with the outcome (despite a terrible churn year)

## Example: Account Segmentation
**The Ask:**
Verchonika wanted to segment the DACH TAL by Bow Tie Stage, Industry, and Revenue.

**Questions to challenge:**
- Why?
- So what?
- 600 accounts → does it make sense to split into 32 tiny groups? (No!)

**What I delivered:**
- TAL by Bow Tie Stage → much simpler and faster

## Step by Step
1. Listen to what the business is asking for
2. Ask questions and challenge
3. Define the problem in plain English
   - What is the problem?
   - So what?
4. Produce the simplest, fastest way to solve the problem
5. Present the solution
6. Gather feedback
7. Repeat
